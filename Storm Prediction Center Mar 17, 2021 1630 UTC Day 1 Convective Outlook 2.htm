   ...SUMMARY...
   A significant episode of severe weather is underway in the
   Ohio River Valley and upper Great Lakes region, with all
   types of severe weather being possible including tornadoes
   (some strong to potentially violent), hurricane-force
   damaging wind gusts, and large hail. The primary area of
   concern will be in east central Indiana into northwestern
   Ohio this afternoon into this evening.

   ...Ohio River Valley...
   - SIGNIFICANT OUTBREAK OF SEVERE THUNDERSTORMS AND TORNADOES
   ANTICIPATED THIS AFTERNOON INTO THE OVERNIGHT HOURS ACROSS
   LARGE SWATHS OF IN/OH/MI -
   
   The primary change to the High Risk outline this outlook is to
   expand the risk slightly in all directions based on recent
   observational trends. The underlying probabilities have also
   been increased with the addition of a 45% tornado risk and a
   60% damaging wind risk over a large swath of E IN/W OH. A
   dangerous, long-duration tornado outbreak is expected to
   begin this afternoon and persist into the overnight hours with
   several rounds of heightened tornado potential.

   The discussions below will cover each forecast wave of severe
   weather:

   Round 1 - Early afternoon severe weather (17Z-22Z): Current
   satellite and radar imagery shows developing showers and
   elevated thunderstorms over parts of east central Illinois
   and into west central Indiana in a highly sheared and
   unstable atmosphere. Forecast soundings from Indianapolis IN
   and Paris IL indicate an extremely unstable atmosphere with
   CAPE values approaching or exceeding 2,500 J/kg, 0-6 km shear
   exceeding 75 kt and long looped hodographs. These factors all
   seem to play into an environment which will favor supercells
   capable of producing tornadoes as the early afternoon hours
   progress and as a very strong low-level jet reaches an ideal
   position for storm development. 0-3 km storm helicities of
   450-600 M2/S2 will be favorable for any supercells that do
   develop to produce strong (EF2-3), potentially long-track to
   very long-track (covering over 50 miles of ground) tornadoes.
   These tornadoes will likely also move at fairly high speeds,
   likely being able to cover 15-20 miles of ground in less than
   25 minutes. A violent tornado (EF4-5) can also not be ruled
   out in this area should any supercell thunderstorm find itself
   able to take full advantage of the environment in place.

   A current concern is the potential for overinitiation of storms
   during this period, however as it stands the high shear should
   keep the storm initiation in check based on current mesoscale
   model anticipations. As these supercell thunderstorms race
   northeast during the early afternoon hours, they should reach
   the 45% + hatched tornado risk area by 19Z-20Z, where forecast
   soundings indicate that instability in this region will rival
   some of the most significant severe weather events on record
   in this region, with storm relative helicity reaching as high
   as 500-650 M2/S2 and CAPE values exceeding 3,000 J/Kg. A
   moderate capping inversion is place in this region based on
   forecast soundings, and should prevent weaker, surface-based
   storms that develop prior to 19Z from significantly impacting
   the instability in place in this area. The supercells from the
   west should be intense enough to where the capping inversion
   should not have a substantial impact on them as they move into
   the area, and the capping inversion should also start to erode
   coinciding with the arrival of supercell storms this afternoon.

   Any supercell storms that persist and remain discrete in this
   area will be capable of producing fast-moving, strong to violent
   (EF2-5) tornadoes that cover large swathes of ground, with the
   possibility of several very long-track (50+ miles) tornadoes
   in this area. The highest threat for violent tornadoes would be
   east of a Edinburgh IN-Warsaw IN line, although the tornado
   threat with this first round should decrease as the storms
   accelerate east and congeal into a MCS by 22Z in western/central
   Ohio. Behind this round of storms there should be a window for
   rapid destabilization, which should play into a second round
   unfolding later in the evening.

   Round 2 - Late evening-overnight severe weather (02Z-07Z):
   Behind this initial round of severe weather, conditions will begin
   to rapidly clear up in IN/OH and a second round of rapid
   destabilization should take hold which should last until sunset,
   and warm air coming into the region from the south should prevent
   the typical destabilization that follows the loss of daytime
   heating until after 04Z. As a frontal boundary reaches western IN
   around 01Z-02Z, it should allow for a second round of showers and
   thunderstorms to develop in an atmosphere that will remain highly
   sheared, although general instability should be lower in this area
   compared to the first round. Any storms that gain a supercelluar
   structure will be capable of producing tornadoes during this
   period, with the chance for a few strong (EF2-3) and long-track
   tornadoes, although the threat for strong tornadoes will be
   at least somewhat lower during this second round comapred to the
   initial round of storms. However, as the storms continue east,
   they will reach a more favorable environment in eastern IN/western
   OH, and any persistent supercell structure will be capable of
   producing strong tornadoes in this area under the context of a
   still extremely unstable atmosphere.

   A violent tornado (EF4-5) can still not be entirely ruled out
   During this period, especially in northwest Ohio where the initial
   round of supercells may not be as prominent and instability may be
   Substantially higher. The threat for damaging winds will increase
   during this period as well, as a intense squall line/MCS is forecast
   to develop behind the supercell storms, and should pose a threat for
   widespread hurricane-force wind gusts across the high risk area.
   Instability will decrease with eastward extent of the storms, and the
   severe threat should largely come to an end by 07Z.

   ...Elsewhere...
   The threat for severe thunderstorms should exist elsewhere across
